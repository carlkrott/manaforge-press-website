The void powers every station in the Caelus Universe. Every ship. Every system. It's the energy that keeps humanity breathing recycled air in the black. And it has a cost.

*VoidWalker* is the first book in the Echoes of Caelus series — a LitRPG progression story set in a corporate-controlled universe where the technology that keeps you alive is the same technology that's eating you from the inside out.

## The Setup

Jaxson fixes pipes. He replaces filters. He's a maintenance worker on a dying station, which makes him the most expendable person on the roster. Nobody notices him. Nobody cares what he sees.

Until he sees what AetherCorp is pulling through the void.

The corporation owns the void energy supply chain. They own the stations. They own the air people breathe. And deep in the restricted sectors, they're running experiments that fold reality in on itself. Jaxson stumbles into the middle of it. Not because he's special. Because he was fixing a leak in the wrong corridor at the wrong time.

## The System

This is progression fantasy with teeth. The power system is tied to void exposure — the more you interact with the space between dimensions, the more you change. Stats fluctuate. Interfaces pulse with data. The system tracks your thresholds, your surges, your critical cascade events.

But the progression isn't clean. Every upgrade comes with corrosion. Every power spike pushes something else out of alignment. The system doesn't care about balance. It's a protocol running on energy that shouldn't exist, and it's breaking down.

VoidWalker isn't about becoming powerful. It's about surviving the process of becoming something else.

## The Stakes

AetherCorp doesn't send threats. They send cost-benefit analyses. When Jaxson becomes a liability, the corporation doesn't get angry — they get efficient. The response is calibrated. Measured. The kind of corporate violence that comes with a budget spreadsheet attached.

And beyond the corporate politics, something in the void is paying attention. The walls between dimensions are thinning. The energy that powers FTL travel and station life support is attracting attention from things that don't have names. Things that notice when you look at them.

## Why This One Hits Different

Most progression fantasy gives you a power fantasy. VoidWalker gives you a survival horror wrapped in system mechanics. The stats are real. The progression is real. But so is the corrosion spreading through Jaxson's veins every time he syncs with the void core.

If you've read Will Wight's Cradle series and wanted the pacing cranked up with the safety rails removed, this is it. If Django Wexler's military fantasy energy resonates with you, Bruno runs in the same lane with a system overlay.
