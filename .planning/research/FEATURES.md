## Table Stakes

Features users expect from any publisher site. Missing these = readers bounce.

### TS-1: Book Catalogue with Cover Art
**Why expected:** Readers visit to find books. Period.
**Complexity:** Medium
**Observed:** Every single publisher has this. Aethon genres, SAP Library, Wraithmarked collections, Black Library franchise pages.
**Notes:** Book pages must include: cover image, blurb, series info, buy links (Amazon), reading order. Genre/series filtering is standard.

### TS-2: Newsletter Signup with Lead Magnet
**Why expected:** The entire indie publishing business model runs on email lists.
**Complexity:** Low (technically) / Medium (content creation for magnet)
**Observed:** Aethon gives "3 full-length eBooks FREE". SAP gives "10+ FREE titles in Starter Library". Grimdark Magazine gives "free magazine issue". Black Library has newsletter. OwlCrate has email capture. Wraithmarked has subscribe popup.
**Notes:** The free-for-email exchange is universal. For Manaforge, free chapters/art/maps are the natural lead magnet. This is non-negotiable.

### TS-3: Series/Reading Order Pages
**Why expected:** LitRPG/fantasy readers are series obsessives. They need to know what order to read.
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Aethon has a dedicated "Series" top-level nav. SAP has series within Universes. Wraithmarked has series collection pages. Black Library is organized by setting/franchise.
**Notes:** This is especially critical for LitRPG where series are long and interconnected.

### TS-4: New Releases / Coming Soon Section
**Why expected:** Readers want to know what's next.
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Aethon has "Latest Releases" + "Coming Soon" with dates. SAP has "Latest Releases". Black Library has "New & Exclusive".
**Notes:** For Manaforge's monthly release cadence, this is a natural fit. Show the release calendar.

### TS-5: Clear Genre Navigation
**Why expected:** Fantasy readers self-select by subgenre (LitRPG, progression fantasy, epic fantasy, etc).
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Aethon has 8 genre categories in top nav. SAP has genre categories. Black Library by franchise.
**Notes:** At minimum: LitRPG, Progression Fantasy, Epic Fantasy. Can grow as catalogue expands.

### TS-6: About / Imprint Identity
**Why expected:** Readers want to know who's behind the books, especially for a new imprint.
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Every publisher has an About page. Aethon's is personality-driven ("burning/blazing", "we exist for the readers"). SAP is mission-driven ("Great Genre Fiction Done Passionately"). Grimdark has "About Us" with team.
**Notes:** For Manaforge, this is where the AI transparency question lives. Whether to lean in or downplay.

### TS-7: Social Media Links
**Why expected:** Readers want to follow elsewhere.
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Every publisher has social links. Most common: Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, YouTube, Discord.
**Notes:** At minimum: Discord server link, Instagram, Facebook.

### TS-8: Mobile-Responsive Design
**Why expected:** 50%+ of traffic is mobile.
**Complexity:** Medium (design constraint, not a feature per se)
**Observed:** All studied sites are responsive. Many look better on mobile than desktop.
**Notes:** LitRPG readers skew younger and more mobile.

### TS-9: Buy Links to Amazon/KU
**Why expected:** Books are on Amazon. Readers need to get there.
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Every publisher links out to Amazon (and often B&N, Audible). Aethon, SAP, Wraithmarked all have direct buy buttons.
**Notes:** For KU titles, link to KU page specifically. Some readers specifically look for KU availability.

### TS-10: Contact Information
**Why expected:** Readers, reviewers, and potential partners need to reach you.
**Complexity:** Low
**Observed:** Every publisher has a contact page or email. SAP has a form. Aethon has a contact page. Grimdark has contact + submissions separately.
**Notes:** Simple form or email. Don't overthink this.

## Differentiators

Features that set Manaforge Press apart. Not expected, but high-value.

### DIFF-1: AI Transparency Dashboard / Process Page
**Value Proposition:** No indie publisher is transparent about AI generation. Being first is a massive trust differentiator.
**Complexity:** Low-Medium
**Notes:** This is THE unique opportunity. A page or section showing the creative process: AI models used, human editorial oversight, quality pipeline. Could include:
- "Our Process" explainer page
- Before/after editing examples (AI raw → edited final)
- Quality metrics or standards
- FAQ about AI-assisted creation

**Risk assessment:** Two camps exist — "lean in" (be proud, attract curious readers) vs "let quality speak" (don't scare off purists). Recommendation: Start with a subtle process page, not a homepage banner. Let readers discover it organically. The transparency becomes a trust builder, not a marketing hook.

### DIFF-2: Free Supplementary Content Hub
**Value Proposition:** Art, maps, chapters, previews as gated/ungated content. Most publishers only offer free books. Manaforge can offer world-building material.
**Complexity:** Medium
**Notes:** This is underserved in the market. Aethon and SAP give away books. Nobody gives away world-building content (maps, character art, lore pages) as a systematic strategy. For AI-generated work where art can be produced at scale, this is a natural advantage.
- Character galleries with AI-generated portraits
- World maps (AI-generated or hand-crafted)
- Chapter previews / first chapters
- "Lore" pages for series
- Could gate some content behind email signup

### DIFF-3: Release Calendar / Schedule Transparency
**Value Proposition:** Monthly non-conflicting release schedule, publicly visible.
**Complexity:** Low
**Notes:** Aethon shows coming soon with dates but not a structured calendar. SAP shows latest releases. Nobody shows a full forward-looking calendar. With 3-5 pen names on a monthly cadence, Manaforge can show readers exactly when each pen name's next book drops.
- Visual calendar or timeline
- Per-pen-name schedule
- Email reminders for specific series
- RSS/iCal feed for dedicated readers

### DIFF-4: Pen Name Brand Distinction
**Value Proposition:** Each pen name has its own brand voice, but unified under one imprint.
**Complexity:** Medium
**Notes:** No publisher does this with deliberate pen names. SAP has "Universes" (shared worlds with different authors) which is the closest analogue. For Manaforge, each pen name could have:
- Distinct visual identity within the site
- Its own series page with unique tone
- Optional sub-pages or filtered views

### DIFF-5: "Behind the Forge" Content Series
**Value Proposition:** Blog/video content showing how AI + human editing produces books. Educational + marketing.
**Complexity:** Medium-High (ongoing content creation)
**Notes:** This combines DIFF-1 (transparency) with content marketing. Could include:
- Blog posts on the creative process
- Timelapse of book creation
- "How we edit" walkthroughs
- Reader Q&A about AI-assisted writing

### DIFF-6: Community Reading Order / Progression Tracker
**Value Proposition:** Interactive tool where readers track which Manaforge books they've read and get recommendations.
**Complexity:** High
**Notes:** Nobody does this in indie publishing. closest is Goodreads integration. Would require auth, database, etc. Defer to post-MVP.

### DIFF-7: AI-Generated Art Gallery
**Value Proposition:** Showcase the AI art pipeline — character portraits, scenes, world maps.
**Complexity:** Medium
**Notes:** Unique to an AI-generated imprint. Could be both a lead magnet and a traffic draw. AI art galleries get shared on social media. Each book could have an "Art" tab.

## Anti-Features

Features to explicitly NOT build. Common mistakes in this domain.

### ANTI-1: Full E-Commerce Storefront
**Why avoid:** Selling books directly requires inventory, shipping, payment processing, customer service, and returns. Amazon does all this.
**What to do instead:** Link out to Amazon/KU. Link to print-on-demand merch stores (Redbubble, TeePublic, etc). Let them handle fulfillment.
**Observed counterexamples:** Wraithmarked and OwlCrate DO sell direct (Shopify). But they're premium edition / subscription box businesses — that's a different model. SAP has a "Shop" but it's minimal. Aethon uses a subdomain (shop.aethonbooks.com).

### ANTI-2: User Accounts / Login System
**Why avoid:** Complexity for minimal benefit at this scale. Readers don't create accounts on publisher sites unless there's a store.
**What to do instead:** Email newsletter for relationship. Social media for community. No login walls.
**Observed:** Only Wraithmarked and OwlCrate have accounts (because they're stores). Aethon, SAP, Grimdark — no accounts needed.

### ANTI-3: Forums / Message Board
**Why avoid:** Dead forums are worse than no forums. Community management is a full-time job. Ghost towns hurt brand.
**What to do instead:** Discord server for community. Off-site, established platform, doesn't die on your website.
**Observed:** Aethon and SAP both link to Discord. Nobody runs their own forum in 2026.

### ANTI-4: Author Portal / Submissions System
**Why avoid:** Manaforge doesn't accept submissions. It's AI-generated in-house content. A submissions page signals "traditional publisher" which is misleading.
**What to do instead:** If asked, have a brief FAQ explaining the model. Don't build a submissions form.
**Observed:** Aethon, SAP, Grimdark all have submissions pages because they accept outside authors. Manaforge doesn't need this.

### ANTI-5: Book Review System (On-Site)
**Why avoid:** On-site reviews are either empty (bad) or curated (untrustworthy). Amazon/Goodreads already solve this.
**What to do instead:** Link to Amazon reviews. Embed Goodreads widgets if desired. Let review platforms do what they do.
**Observed:** Nobody runs their own review system on publisher sites. Even Black Library doesn't.

### ANTI-6: Individual Pen Name Websites
**Why avoid:** 3-5 separate sites = 3-5x maintenance, 3-5x cost, 3-5x things to forget to update. Centralised is better at this scale.
**What to do instead:** One Manaforge Press site with pen name sections. The pen names are distinct brands within one site, not separate sites.
**Context:** Project context explicitly states "centralised imprint blog (no individual pen name blogs)." This is the right call.

### ANTI-7: Aggressive Pop-ups / Interstitials
**Why avoid:** The indie publishing space is already popup-heavy (newsletter gate on SAP, entry popup on Wraithmarked). Don't be the worst offender.
**What to do instead:** Subtle newsletter capture in sidebar, footer, and dedicated page. Maybe ONE exit-intent popup max.
**Observed:** SAP has a full-page newsletter gate (annoying). Aethon's is more restrained. Be like Aethon.

### ANTI-8: "As Seen On" / Social Proof Bars with Fake Logos
**Why avoid:** Unless you have genuine placement in those outlets, it's obvious and hurts credibility. Especially risky for a new AI-generated imprint where trust is the primary challenge.
**What to do instead:** Real reader reviews, genuine Amazon rankings, actual sales milestones when achieved.

## Overdone Patterns to Avoid

Patterns that are so common they're noise, not signal.

### OVER-1: Generic "New Releases" Grid
**What it is:** Cover art grid with title and "See The Book" link. Every publisher does this identically.
**Why it's overdone:** It's table stakes done boringly. You need it, but don't make it your homepage hero.
**Do instead:** Lead with a featured release that has personality (like Aethon's featured release with blurb), then show the grid below.

### OVER-2: WordPress-Default Book Layout
**What it is:** Standard blog-post-style book pages with cover, text, and buy link. Functional but generic.
**Why it's overdone:** Aethon and SAP both use WordPress and look very similar. The ModFarm Sites platform SAP uses is literally a white-label publisher template.
**Do instead:** Custom book page design with world-building content, art, and series context. Make it feel premium.

### OVER-3: Newsletter Popup on First Visit
**What it is:** "Join our mailing list!" popup within 2 seconds of arriving.
**Why it's overdone:** SAP, Wraithmarked, OwlCrate all do this. Readers are blind to it.
**Do instead:** Time-delayed (30s+), exit-intent, or dedicated landing page only. Better yet: make the lead magnet so good they seek out the signup.

### OVER-4: "Submissions Open" Pages
**What it is:** "We're looking for authors!" page.
**Why it's overdone:** Every publisher has one. Manaforge doesn't accept submissions.
**Do instead:** Don't have one. Replace with a "Our Process" or "About Our Books" page.

### OVER-5: Generic Taglines
**What it is:** "The next generation of Science Fiction & Fantasy" (Aethon) or "Great Genre Fiction Done Passionately" (SAP).
**Why it's overdone:** These say nothing unique. Every publisher thinks they're the next generation.
**Do instead:** Manaforge's tagline should hint at the AI/forge/creation angle. "Forged by Imagination. Powered by AI." or similar.

## Unique Opportunities for AI-Generated Imprint

These are specifically enabled by the AI-generation angle:

### OPP-1: Rapid Content Production Showcase
AI enables faster book production. The website can showcase this with a visible, growing catalogue that updates monthly — something most indie publishers can't match at this pace.

### OPP-2: AI Art Pipeline as Content
Every book generates art (covers, character portraits, maps). This art is both a byproduct and a marketing asset. Most publishers commission art once. Manaforge can produce art at scale and use it as traffic-driving content.

### OPP-3: Transparency as Trust Hack
In a market where AI content is feared/hidden, radical transparency is a differentiator. A clear, honest "How We Make Books" page can:
- Build trust with skeptical readers
- Attract curiosity traffic from the "AI book" discourse
- Position Manaforge as a leader in a nascent category

### OPP-4: Cross-Pen-Name Universe Building
AI makes it easier to maintain consistency across pen names. The site could subtly connect pen name worlds (shared easter eggs, lore crossovers) in a way that rewards dedicated readers — similar to SAP's "Universes" but with a single creative intelligence behind it.

### OPP-5: Interactive World-Building
AI can generate content on demand. The site could eventually offer:
- AI-generated character backstories on request
- Interactive maps that expand as new books release
- "What if" scenarios or alternate endings voted on by readers

These are speculative and Phase 3+, but they represent genuinely unique opportunities that traditional publishers literally cannot execute.
